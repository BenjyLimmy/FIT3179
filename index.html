Global Fuel Emissions and Air Pollution

A Deep Dive into Trends and Malaysia's Impact

Global Overview

Global Air Pollution Deaths Over Time

This map highlights global air pollution deaths per 100,000 people. In 2023, African countries show significantly higher death rates, while Asia sees moderate figures, and North America experiences lower numbers. Historically, these numbers increased before a global decline leading to 2023. The drop in pollution deaths can be linked to global policies aimed at improving air quality and reducing industrial emissions.

Global Fuel Emissions per Capita

Countries with large oil reserves and extraction industries, such as Libya, Kazakhstan, and Canada, see above-average fuel emissions. Certain countries like Libya exhibit high emissions due to fossil fuel reliance for energy generation. Notably, India has one of the lowest emissions per capita, largely due to its lower industrial output relative to its large population and significant use of renewable energy sources. Saudi Arabia continues to see high levels of sulphur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions due to its large-scale oil industry.

Economic Factors

Economic Power vs Environmental Impact

Explore the balance between wealth and environmental impact. Singapore and Switzerland boast some of the highest GDPs with moderate fuel emissions, despite their small populations. On the other hand, Venezuela exhibits a low GDP but high fuel emissions. Malaysia ranks above average in fuel emissions, with a mid-range GDP. There is a noticeable correlation between GDP and fuel emissions, though exceptions like Greenland and Venezuela reveal other influential factors such as economic instability and energy dependence.

Asia accounts for more than half of global fuel emissions, with biomass as a primary fuel source. This underscores the region's energy demand and the limited penetration of renewable alternatives in rural and industrial sectors. In North America, light oil dominates the emissions breakdown, reflecting its highly developed transportation infrastructure and extensive use of personal vehicles.

Proportional Breakdown by Emission Type in Malaysia

Zooming into Malaysia's emission types, it is observed that the largest contributor to emissions in Malaysia is carbon monoxide, followed by NMVOC. These emissions result from transportation and industrial activities, indicating the country's dependency on fossil fuels. The high carbon monoxide levels reflect Malaysia's reliance on gasoline for vehicles, while NMVOC emissions come from industrial solvents and manufacturing.

Trends & Future Projections

Shifting Gears: Global and Malaysia Fuel Emissions Trends (2013-2023)

Time Trends Chart

Both globally and in Malaysia, fuel emissions rose steadily until the sharp drop in 2020, primarily due to the global slowdown in transportation and industrial operations during the COVID-19 pandemic. With global fuel emissions continuing to decline, forecasts suggest this trend could persist as more nations commit to paving the way for a greener future.
The post-pandemic recovery in Malaysia saw a sustained decrease in emissions, likely reflecting structural changes, such as adoption of electric vehicles and an accelerating shift toward cleaner, renewable energy sources, signaling a long-term transformation in national energy consumption.

Malaysia's Fuel Emission Trends by Source

Looking closer at Malaysia's total emissions, light oil is Malaysia's main emission source, followed by industrial processes. Emissions peaked in 2017 due to a 35% rise in light oil use, driven by industrial growth. The dip in 2015 coincides with global oil price drops and reduced industrial output. We see that Malaysia's emissions are strongly tied to economic shifts, particularly in the oil market and industrial expansion.
